§1  Executive Summary &amp; Strategic Intent
The HR-X Intelligence Hub is the enterprise B2B product at the center of SHRM&apos;s repositioning from a manual assessment and consulting vendor to a scalable, data-driven workforce decision intelligence provider. It is simultaneously the most commercially advanced ILS project and the most architecturally complex — a five-mode platform serving two distinct user populations across a three-tier intelligence model.
The Hub is built on three proprietary assets SHRM already owns: the HR-X Assessment (a validated 95-item diagnostic instrument across 4 dimensions, 16 practice areas, and 71 subpractice areas); the SHRM BASK (the authoritative Body of Applied Skills and Knowledge, serving as the content standard for all prescriptive recommendations); and SHRM Member and Workforce Data (longitudinal survey history, behavioral telemetry, and verified professional identity across 330,000+ members, forming the foundation of the benchmark panel and the VoW Synthetic simulation engine).
Strategic Reframe
A maturity score tells an enterprise where they are. The HR-X Solution Hub tells them where they are, what to do about it, and — uniquely — lets them simulate what happens if they act. That progression — Diagnostic → Prescriptive → Predictive — is the architecture of the platform and the logic of the commercial offer.
The Hub is currently in market as a manually operated consulting engagement (the MVP), generating revenue with named enterprise pilot clients including Amazon, MasterCard, Progressive, Albemarle, and Cooper&apos;s Hawk. The MVP validates that enterprise HR leaders will pay for a structured, SHRM-grounded diagnostic with a guided results conversation. The Hub is the scalable architecture that makes what the solutions team does today available to 10x the enterprise client volume without 10x the headcount.
This project definition establishes the Hub as a full ILS project (ILS-XHUB-01), aligns its architecture to the strategic principles governing the Intelligence Layer Studio, and appends a three-phase VoC research plan covering needs discovery, concept validation, and usability testing across the Hub&apos;s primary user populations.

2.2  Proprietary Intelligence Boundary — Non-Negotiable Principle
Regardless of whether SHRM builds the Hub internally, licenses a third-party platform, or pursues a hybrid architecture, one principle governs every architectural decision: SHRM&apos;s proprietary intelligence must never reside inside a vendor&apos;s platform.
The Proprietary Intelligence Boundary: SHRM&apos;s scoring engine, vectorized BASK corpus, benchmark panel data, member longitudinal data, and VoW Synthetic persona infrastructure constitute the moat that makes the Hub defensible. These assets live in SHRM&apos;s own data layer. Any vendor platform accesses SHRM intelligence via API. The vendor provides the delivery shell, the UX, and the visualization layer. SHRM retains the intelligence. A vendor who requires SHRM&apos;s data to live in their platform is structurally disqualifying regardless of other capabilities.

§7  Open Architecture Questions for Leadership Resolution
The following items require explicit decision before Phase 1 development proceeds. They are not resolvable at the ILS level alone.
Platform build vs. license decision
The Proprietary Intelligence Boundary is architectural, not preferential. The decision about whether to build the delivery shell internally, license a third-party platform (Qualtrics, Perceptyx, Alchemer, ThoughtSpot), or pursue a hybrid depends on vendor capability to consume external APIs for scoring rather than implementing scoring logic natively. This capability must be the first evaluation criterion in any vendor assessment.
Approval authority model (Mode 1)
Who on the SHRM Business team can publish deliverables to enterprise clients, and whether a second-reviewer is required for certain account tiers, is a SHRM Business governance decision. The platform should support configurable approval tiers; the default should require Solutions Architect approval for all publishes.
BASK crosswalk to HR-X subpractices
A formal empirical crosswalk between the BASK competency taxonomy and HR-X practice area/subpractice structure has been identified as a gap. The BASK-grounded intervention library in Tier 2 depends on this mapping. Charles Glover&apos;s team owns this dependency.
Temporal / change architecture
The Hub currently measures HR maturity at a point in time. The re-assessment cadence creates longitudinal data, but no formal mechanism exists for detecting and surfacing maturity changes that occur between assessment periods. This architectural gap should be addressed before Tier 2 design is finalized.
Team-level unit of analysis
The Hub currently operates at the organizational level (via the HR-X instrument) and the individual level (via BASK credentialing). A team-level unit of analysis — sitting between individual practitioner assessment and whole-organization HR maturity — has been identified as a gap relevant to enterprise buyer needs. Scoping this as a future module should be a Phase 2 design decision.
AI disruption as first-class capability dimension
Neither the HR-X framework nor the BASK taxonomy currently addresses AI disruption as a distinct HR capability domain. Given the pace of AI adoption in HR functions (69.8% weekly AI use, only 7.3% with enterprise-approved AI tools), this represents a credibility gap in the framework that will surface in buyer conversations. Formal treatment of AI capability in the Hub&apos;s prescriptive recommendations should be scoped before Tier 2 launch.

R1  Research Rationale &amp; Scope
The HR-X Solution Hub serves two distinct user populations across five operating modes. Research must address both populations — the enterprise buyer and decision-maker (CHRO and HR leadership) and the SHRM Business operator (Solutions Architects, Customer Success Managers) — because each population has distinct needs, distinct success criteria, and distinct usability requirements. The research plan is structured to address both in sequence, prioritizing the enterprise buyer in the needs and concept phases (where insight is most scarce) and incorporating SHRM internal users more heavily in usability testing (where access is greatest).
The Hub also has an existing proof point in the market — the MVP. This is both an asset and a constraint: it means some needs-level data already exists from pilot engagement debriefs, but it also means concept testing must be designed carefully to separate reactions to the current MVP from reactions to the Hub&apos;s expanded capability set.

R3  Phase 1: Needs Discovery
Phase Status: ACTIVE
Phase 1 establishes the foundational needs baseline for both user populations. It uses a qualitative-led approach — in-depth interviews as the primary method — supplemented by a targeted quantitative survey to validate directional findings at scale. Because the SHRM Business operator population is internal and fully accessible, it is treated as the priority population for immediate research initiation.
R3.1  Research Questions — Enterprise Buyer Population
How do CHROs currently assess their own HR function&apos;s maturity, and how formal or informal is that process?
What vendors, frameworks, or internal processes do enterprise HR leaders currently use to benchmark their HR function against peers? What do they find credible, and what do they distrust?
What would motivate a CHRO to shift from a point-in-time consulting engagement to a retained annual subscription relationship for HR maturity guidance?
What level of evidence does a CHRO require before acting on an assessment finding — and who else in the organization needs to be convinced (CFO, CEO, business unit heads)?
What are the most frequent blockers to acting on the prioritized recommendations that come out of an assessment engagement?
How does the AI disruption dimension factor into how CHROs think about HR capability gaps today?
What is the CHRO&apos;s experience with competitive offerings (PwC Saratoga, Mercer, McLean)? Where do those offerings fall short?

R3.2  Research Questions — SHRM Business Operator Population
In the current manual MVP process, where does the Solutions Architect spend the most time, and where does the process create the most friction?
What does the Solutions Architect currently add to the AI-generated narrative that could not be automated — and what parts of the current manual process could be automated without losing quality?
What account signals currently go undetected because there is no systematic tracking mechanism — and what would a Solutions Architect want to be alerted to automatically?
How does the CSM currently monitor account health across the book of business, and what makes an account feel &apos;at risk&apos; vs. &apos;progressing&apos;?
What information from the Discovery Audit (Appendix A) is most impactful in improving the quality of the assessment output — and what is hardest to get from enterprise clients?

R6.2  Method
Parameter
Specification
Target population
Two separate test populations: (1) CHRO / HR leadership for Mode 2b testing; (2) Solutions Architects and CSMs for Mode 1 and 3a testing. SHRM internal operators are accessible immediately; enterprise user testing requires participant recruitment.
Format
Moderated think-aloud usability sessions. 60–75 minutes. Screen-shared remote sessions (Teams/Zoom). Facilitator + observer. Recordings with consent.
Sample size
n=6–8 per user population (standard usability threshold for issue saturation). Two rounds planned: Round 1 after initial prototype; Round 2 after design iteration.
Recruitment
Enterprise users: SHRM Executive Network and warm referrals via solutions team. Internal users: direct recruitment from SHRM Business team.
Task scenarios
Mode 2b: cold-start navigation tasks (find dimension score; compare to financial services peers; identify top-priority gap; navigate to SHRM product recommendation). NLQ tasks (ask 3 pre-defined questions; evaluate response accuracy and usefulness). Mode 1: end-to-end publish workflow (review scores; edit 2 narrative sections; approve / suppress 3 product recommendations; publish). Mode 3a: account health triage (identify top 3 at-risk accounts; locate next best action for each).
Metrics
Task completion rate; time-on-task; error rate; System Usability Scale (SUS) score; qualitative friction points and delight moments.
Validation threshold
SUS score ≥68 (industry-standard &apos;good&apos; threshold) across both user populations after design iteration round. Critical task completion rate ≥85% without facilitator assistance.
Timeline
Initiated when Phase 1 platform build has a testable Mode 2b prototype (estimated M6–M9 of platform build).

R6.3  UX Testing Governance
All usability findings are documented in a standardized Issue Log categorized by severity (Critical / Major / Minor) and user population (Enterprise / SHRM Internal). Critical issues block platform release. Major issues are addressed before external client access. Minor issues are prioritized in the next sprint cycle. The Issue Log is maintained by the Product team and reviewed at each Phase gate with SHRM Business and Product leadership.

Synthesis cadence
Phase 1 findings: interim summary at Week 4 (qualitative only); full synthesis at Week 12 (qualitative + quantitative). Phase 2 findings: synthesis report within 4 weeks of final session. Phase 3: findings report within 2 weeks of each usability round.
Existing data credit
All existing SHRM data assets credited in §R2 are documented with sample details and fieldwork dates. Where existing data is credited as satisfying a needs question, new primary research is not commissioned for that question unless Phase 1 interviews surface material contradictions.
Research disclosure
Scale Free Strategy&apos;s involvement in research design and analysis is disclosed to Alex Alonso proactively. Research methodology documentation notes the dual role.
VoW Synthetic note
VoW Synthetic is not used to substitute for primary research in any phase of this plan. Once VoW Synthetic achieves Phase 0 validation (90% accuracy benchmark), it may be used to supplement quantitative sample where enterprise panel cells fall below N=30 minimum — this will be documented explicitly when it occurs and will not be represented as primary human research.
